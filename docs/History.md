<h2>HISTORY.MD: The Moral Obligation of the Witness</h2>

<p>
This document contextualizes the <strong>Sovereign Field Operator</strong> not as a consumer device, but as a successor to 20th-century forensic preservation practices. Recording is treated as a duty to truth.
</p>

<hr>

<h3>1. The Smithsonian Legacy</h3>

<p>
Moses Asch and the Smithsonian-Folkways project treated all sound as historical record.
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Non-Interference:</strong> No beautification; raw signal only.</li>
  <li><strong>Artifact:</strong> Physical media preserves beyond memory decay.</li>
</ul>

<hr>

<h3>2. Studs Terkel</h3>

<p>
Studs Terkel used recording to document lived experience outside official narratives.
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Haptic Presence:</strong> The recording device establishes a boundary of truth.</li>
  <li><strong>Temporal Detail:</strong> Captures rhythm, pause, and environment.</li>
</ul>

<hr>

<h3>3. The A = A Directive</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Identity:</strong> A recording must equal the event.</li>
  <li><strong>Rejection of Simulation:</strong> No approximation or modeling.</li>
  <li><strong>Obligation:</strong> Distortion or loss is a failure of record.</li>
</ul>
